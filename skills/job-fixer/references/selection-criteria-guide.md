# Selection Criteria Guide

Selection criteria are formal competency assessments used in government, academic, and many
corporate roles — especially in Australia, UK, Canada, and NZ. Each criterion must be addressed
individually with evidence. A weak or generic response is disqualifying.

---

## What Selection Criteria Really Test

Each criterion is testing a specific competency. Before writing, decode what they're actually
asking:

| Criterion phrasing | What they want to see |
|---|---|
| "Demonstrated ability to..." | A real past example with measurable outcome |
| "Experience in..." | Depth and recency of relevant work |
| "Commitment to..." | Values alignment + behaviour under pressure |
| "Ability to work in a team..." | Collaboration skills, conflict resolution |
| "Strong communication skills" | Clarity, adaptability to audience, written + verbal |
| "Analytical and problem-solving" | Structured thinking, data use, decision-making |

---

## The STAR Framework

Use STAR for every criterion that asks for demonstrated or proven ability:

**S — Situation**: Brief context. Where, when, what was the challenge?
**T — Task**: What was your specific responsibility in that situation?
**A — Action**: What did YOU do? (Use "I", not "we") Be specific about your decisions and steps.
**R — Result**: What was the outcome? Quantify if possible. What did you learn?

### Good STAR example:
> **S:** During a product launch at [Company], our QA pipeline was failing 30% of nightly builds
> due to unstable test infrastructure. **T:** I was tasked with reducing failures to under 5%
> without delaying the release. **A:** I audited all failing tests, isolated flaky tests caused
> by network timeouts, and introduced retry logic and mock services for external APIs.
> **R:** Build failure rate dropped to 2% within two weeks and the release shipped on time,
> with zero post-launch test regressions.

### Weak example to avoid:
> "I have strong QA skills and always ensure quality in my work. I am a team player who
> communicates well with stakeholders."

## Length Guidelines

| Application type | Per criterion |
|---|---|
| Government (APS, Civil Service) | 250–400 words |
| Academic / Research | 200–350 words |
| Corporate / startup | 150–250 words |
| Brief "rate yourself" forms | 2–4 sentences + one example |

---

## Handling Weak Evidence

If the user's profile doesn't have strong evidence for a criterion:

1. **Use transferable experience** — find the closest analogue from a different domain
2. **Use smaller examples** — a minor project is better than vague claims
3. **Be honest about development** — "While I haven't led a team of 10+, I have [X]. I am
   actively developing this by [Y]."
4. **Never fabricate** — an honest partial match is better than a lie

## Common Criterion Types and How to Tackle Them

### Technical/Domain Skills
- Lead with the most relevant project or role
- Name specific tools, methods, outputs
- Quantify scale or impact

### Communication
- Give an example with a specific audience (executive, technical, non-technical)
- Show you adapted your style
- Mention format (presentation, written report, workshop, etc.)

### Leadership / Stakeholder Management
- Name who you led or influenced (internal team, client, cross-functional)
- Describe a moment of difficulty or disagreement and how you resolved it

### Initiative / Problem Solving
- Show you identified the problem yourself (not just responded to a task)
- Describe your reasoning process
- Highlight what was innovative or non-obvious

### Diversity / Inclusion / Equity
- Reference working with people from different backgrounds
- Show empathy and adaptability
- Mention processes or practices you improved or advocated for

## Format for Output

For each criterion, output:

```markdown
### [Criterion number / title]

**Key competency being assessed:** [1 sentence interpretation]

**My evidence:**
[STAR-formatted response, 200–400 words]

**Strength of evidence:** ✅ Strong / ⚠️ Partial / ❌ Gap — [honest note if weak]
```
