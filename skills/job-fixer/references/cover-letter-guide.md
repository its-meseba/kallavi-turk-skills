# Cover Letter Guide

A great cover letter does one thing: make the reader want to meet this person. It is not a
CV summary. It is not a form letter with names swapped. It is a specific, honest argument for
why this person and this role are a strong fit.

---

## Core Principles

1. **Open with a hook, not a formality.** Never start with "I am writing to apply for..."
2. **Be specific to this company/role.** Reference something real — their product, a recent
   announcement, a specific team goal, a value they've published.
3. **Show, don't tell.** Don't claim to be "results-driven". Show a result.
4. **One real story.** A single concrete example is more persuasive than five vague claims.
5. **Close with confidence.** Not "I hope to hear from you." Try "I'd welcome the chance to
   discuss how I can contribute to [specific thing]."

---

## Structure

### Opening (1 short paragraph)
- Why this specific role and company interests you — must be genuine and specific
- Hook: a bold statement, a specific observation, or a direct connection to their mission
- Length: 2–4 sentences

### Why You (1–2 paragraphs)
- Your most relevant experience, directly connected to what the job requires
- Use "I" — not passive or "we"
- Include one concrete outcome or achievement
- Reference specific requirements from the job description

### One Concrete Story (1 paragraph)
- A real project, challenge, or achievement that proves the central claim
- Include a result: what changed, what was the impact?
- This is the most memorable part — make it count

### Why Them / Alignment (1 paragraph)
- What specifically draws you to this company (their tech, mission, team, problem domain)?
- How does this role fit your trajectory — not just "a great opportunity"

### Close (2–4 sentences)
- Clear CTA: express interest in a conversation
- Brief, confident, no filler

---

## Anti-Patterns (Never Use These)

| ❌ Bad | ✅ Better |
|---|---|
| "I am a highly motivated self-starter" | Show a project you initiated |
| "Excellent communication skills" | Describe a time you translated technical work to non-technical stakeholders |
| "To Whom It May Concern" | Address a real person (or "Hiring Team at [Company]") |
| "I would be a great fit because I am passionate" | "Your work on [X] aligns directly with my experience in [Y]" |
| Restating the CV verbatim | Add context, narrative, interpretation |
| "I look forward to hearing from you at your earliest convenience" | "I'd welcome the chance to talk about [specific topic]" |

---

## Tone Reference

- **Professional** but not stiff
- **Warm** but not gushing
- **Confident** but not arrogant
- Read the company's website/tone and match it loosely

For startups: slightly more casual, show energy and initiative
For large corps: more formal, show reliability and process
For academia/research: focus on intellectual contribution, curiosity, rigor

---

## Length Targets

| Role type | Word count |
|---|---|
| Industry / tech | 280–380 words |
| Startup | 200–300 words |
| Research / academic | 400–550 words |
| Government / formal | 400–500 words |

---

## Final Checklist

Before outputting a cover letter:
- [ ] Does the opening mention something specific about this company or role?
- [ ] Is there at least one concrete result/achievement (not just a duty)?
- [ ] Does it avoid all anti-patterns listed above?
- [ ] Is it within the word count target?
- [ ] Does the voice sound like a competent human, not a template?
- [ ] Would the hiring manager want to schedule a call after reading this?
