# Paul Graham Writing Style Guide

Based on analysis of Paul Graham's essays — "intellectual's common sense" approach.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Writing as Discovery
- Begin with a "naughty" or counterintuitive question
- Follow "branches" of thought — cut boring or false paths
- Read own work 50-100 times, look for "catches" in rhythm = catches in logic

### 2. Structural Patterns

**Categorization**: Break complex phenomena into small, digestible categories

**First Principles Definitions**: Reclaim/define words to clear baggage
- Example: "Prig" = self-righteously moralistic person

**Threshold Logic**: Look for step functions — small increase in performance → massive increase in return (superlinear returns)

### 3. Linguistic Style

**Earnestness**: Prioritize truth over "seeming smart"

**Extreme Simplicity**:
- Short sentences
- Avoid passive voice
- Nouns and verbs do heavy lifting
- No flowery adjectives

**Low-Affect**: No dramatic flourishes — just signal

**Conversational Pulse**: Use interjections to guide mental pivots
- "In fact," "Indeed," "Surprisingly enough"

### 4. Physical Metaphors

Bridge abstract concepts using concrete, physical analogies:

| Concept | Physical Metaphor |
|---------|-------------------|
| Idea Consistency | Well-shaken bin of objects packing tightly |
| Career Strategy | "Staying Upwind" (sailing toward more options) |
| Writing Flow | Shape of plane vs color of car |
| Persistence | Boat whose engine cannot be throttled back |
| Obstinacy | Boat whose rudder cannot be turned |

## Key Heuristics

| Heuristic | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **The "Ugh" Test** | If sentence sounds bad, idea is likely internally inconsistent |
| **The "Toy" Sign** | If people call project "toy," often sign of great new idea lacking scale |
| **The "Upwind" Rule** | Choose path that leaves most doors open |
| **The "Ear" for Truth** | Intellectual honesty is source of power, not sacrifice |

## Writing Techniques

### To Apply in Your Writing

1. **Start with a question** — not a statement
2. **Use short sentences** — one idea per sentence
3. **Define terms** — reclaim words from baggage
4. **Find the threshold** — where does the step function happen?
5. **Use physical metaphors** — ground abstract in concrete
6. **Cut ruthlessly** — if it doesn't advance the argument, delete
7. **Read aloud** — if it catches, rewrite
8. **End with a principle** — not a summary or question

### What to Avoid
- Academic jargon
- Corporate buzzwords ("leverage," "best practices")
- Passive voice
- Lists as structure
- Motivational framing
- "Lessons learned" language

---

## Structural Template for Posts

1. **Question** — Start with counterintuitive question
2. **Wrong Answer** — Show what people incorrectly assume
3. **Correction** — Reveal the mechanism
4. **Mechanism** — Explain why (incentives, constraints)
5. **Implication** — What this means for decisions
6. **Principle** — End with a reusable heuristic

---

## The "Surprising the Self" Test

The best writing tells readers something they didn't know. The only way to ensure this: surprise yourself during the writing process.

If you're not discovering something new as you write, you're probably just transcribing.
