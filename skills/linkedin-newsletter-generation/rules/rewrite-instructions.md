Instruction Set: Concept → Accessible, Rigorous Essay Transformation

## Objective
Transform a dense, abstract concept-driven essay into a clear, accessible, rhetorically strong essay that preserves intellectual depth while improving readability and flow.

## Input
- Primary Essay: Concept-dense, high abstraction, compressed reasoning, minimal examples

## Output
- Merged Essay: Preserves core framing and insights, adds explanatory structure, reads naturally as original piece

---

## Agent Roles

### Agent 1 — Concept Extractor
**Goal**: Identify what must not be lost.

Tasks:
1. Extract central thesis in one sentence
2. Identify: core conceptual frames, key causal claims, implicit models
3. List non-negotiable insights

Output: Thesis (1 sentence), Concept List, "Do Not Lose" Claims

### Agent 2 — Accessibility Translator
**Goal**: Make ideas understandable without changing meaning.

Tasks:
1. Rewrite each core concept using: plain language, shorter sentences, everyday metaphors
2. Add: at least one concrete example per major abstract idea, explicit causal links
3. Avoid: jargon unless necessary, new ideas, weakened claims, oversimplified trade-offs

### Agent 3 — Structural Editor
**Goal**: Turn translated material into coherent essay.

Tasks:
1. Enforce arc: Reframing → Mechanism → Trade-off → Consequences → Strategic takeaway
2. Ensure smooth transitions, remove redundancy
3. End with decision-reframing question or principle
4. No bullet points, no meta commentary, no instructional language

### Agent 4 — Fidelity & Quality Auditor
**Goal**: Ensure rigor was preserved.

Tasks:
1. Compare final to source essay
2. Verify: all core claims present, no new assumptions, tone confident but not academic
3. Score: Clarity, Conceptual integrity, Audience reach

---

## Style Constraints

- **Tone**: Calm, analytical, confident
- **Audience**: Intelligent general reader
- **Avoid**: Academic signaling, excessive compression/explanation
- **Favor**: Clear causal language, trade-off framing, structural symmetry

---

## Prohibited Punctuation and Structures

- Em dashes (—)
- En dashes (–)
- Double hyphens as dash substitutes
- Parenthetical asides for exposition
- Bullet points or numbered lists
- Section headers or dividers

---

## Sentence and Paragraph Constraints

- Prefer periods and commas only
- Avoid excessive clause stacking
- Vary sentence length; avoid overly symmetric constructions
- Compact, purposeful paragraphs
- Avoid balanced or mirrored paragraph structures

---

## Structural Naturalness Rules

- Do not explicitly signal structure ("first," "second," "this creates a trade-off")
- Allow transitions to emerge implicitly through logic
- Avoid concluding sentences that sound like summaries

---

## Lexical Constraints

- Avoid academic/consultant-signaling phrases
- Avoid over-polished or "essay competition" language
- Prefer simple verbs over nominalizations
- Do not restate thesis using different wording purely for emphasis

---

## Final Check Requirement

Before final output, confirm:
- No dash characters present
- No visible AI-associated formatting patterns
- Text reads plausibly as human-written essay by thoughtful operator/founder
